Take it to the Next Level

You have probably heard of cloud computing at some point. Google Docs was the first cloud computing service to achieve widespread acceptance among the public. As a SaaS (Software as a Service) application, Docs offers several advantages over traditional word-processing applications. It doesn’t offer all of the features of a very mature and robust application like Microsoft Word, yet it does provide a rich toolset that is good enough for most word processing and layout tasks. For example, this document was created using Google Docs.

The main advantages of Google Docs are:

  • no software installation required
  • available anywhere with an internet connection
  • free
  • simple
  • works hand-in-glove with google Drive and other Google products

This article is directed at two groups of people: people who have no experience with Google Docs, and those who have some experience, but who haven’t explored some of the features that make it more than just a text editor. For those with previous Docs experience, you may want to skip ahead to the next section.


Sign in to Google Docs

Right away, we can see that there’s a difference between Google Docs and traditional word processors. Why do you need to sign in? Because, remember, Google Docs is SaaS, not software residing on your computer. When you use Google Docs, you interact with software on Google’s servers, using your browser as an interface.

Choose your Fonts

Google Docs draws on an extensive library of open-source fonts. These fonts are extremely popular and Google’s font libraries are often drawn upon by web designers. Chances are good that you’ve seen at least one Google font (and probably dozens) while browsing the web today. Instead of downloading and installing fonts to your computer, you can use the fonts tool in Docs to import desired fonts from Google’s libraries.


Use the Fonts Menu



By default, you will be using the Ariel font. To import additional fonts, click on the Fonts menu and select More Fonts.

Search and Browse


Selecting More Fonts from the Fonts menu takes you to the font library.

Here, you can search for and browse fonts. Because so many fonts are available, it's a good idea to narrow the field according to your interests. For example, if you’re only interested in sans serif fonts (most useful for headings and titles, less useful for large bodies of text), you would click the Show: All fonts button and select the category Sans Serif.

Import a Font



Let’s say you want a futuristic-looking font. You scroll down the list of fonts and notice one named Orbitron that seems to suit your needs. To add that font to your list of fonts, click it. A check mark will appear next to the font, indicating that it is ready to be imported into your font list. If you wish, you can select multiple fonts the same way at this point. When you’re done, click the OK button to return to your document and start using your new fonts.

Add and Resize Images

Adding and manipulating images to your documents will help them be more visually pleasing and easier to read by breaking up the monotony of a text-only document.

Get Some Images



Before we get started with this section, make sure you have some images on your computer. If you don’t have any images, download some royalty-free images from Creative Commons, then keep track of where you keep these images. We will ask you to upload an image from your computer and place it in your document.

Note that, in general, you will get the best results from larger images. If you have images that have already been cropped or resized to a very small area, the quality of these images will suffer if you attempt to enlarge them in your document. On the other hand, if you have an image that is too large to fit in the available area of your document, it will be automatically resized when you embed it with no loss in quality.

So remember: when dealing with digital images, it is possible to make them smaller with no loss in quality. However, it is not possible to make images larger without a loss in quality.

Insert an Image



First, place your cursor at the place in the document where you want your image to appear. Then click on the Insert menu, then select Image.

The Insert Image dialog will open. The default actions available from this dialog are to either drag an image into the document or navigate to the image’s location on your computer. You may find one method easier than another. In this tutorial, we’ll drag our image into the window.

Side note: there are several other options available through this interface, including linking to externally-hosted images and capturing an image through your webcam. We encourage you to explore these other methods of integrating images into your document once we’re done.

Select and Drag



On your computer, locate an image you’d like to embed in your document, then drag it into the Insert Image window. Below, we’ve selected the image “wyoming-evening.jpg” and are about to drag it into the Insert Image window.


As soon as you drag your image into the Insert Image window, your image will be uploaded to Google Docs and embedded in your document. Notice that the image will be resized to automatically fit within your document’s margins.


Resize

Suppose you don’t want your image to take up so much space in your document. No problem. You can resize images after they have been embedded in your document. You can also change the way text flows around your images..

Let’s add some more text to your document, then resize the image, then change the way our text flows around our image.

Add More Text



Begin by adding a paragraph of text below your image. In the example below, we’ve added dummy text generated here.

Once you’ve added your text, it’s time to resize your image. Start by clicking once on the image. You will notice that a border appears around your image, and that the border is intersected by eight small squares: one at each corner and one in the middle of each side. Additionally, a line extends up from the top center of the image and is connected to a dot, as shown below. What are these squares and dot for? 


Get a Handle on Things


The squares surrounding the image shown above are for resizing the image in different ways, and the dot  is for rotating the image. These squares and dot are called handles. To resize your image while keeping the proportions of the image the same, we’ll use a corner handle. To resize the image without keeping the proportions the same, we’ll use a side handle.

Let’s start with the most common task: changing the size of the image while retaining the proportions of the original image. Click on any corner handle and drag that handle toward the center of the image. As you do this, the border around the image will shrink, indicating the target size of the image. Let’s drag our corner handle until we have selected a target size about 25% of our original size.

Now the width of our resized image takes up about half of the width it used to. Because our image, like our text, is left-aligned by default, it sits on the left side of the page.

Messed up? No Problem.



Because placing and aligning text and images can entail some trial-and-error, it’s important to recall that if you make mistakes, you can undo your previous actions by clicking the Undo button in the upper left corner or by using the keyboard shortcut control+z.

For example, if we want our image to be center-aligned instead of left-aligned as shown above, we click the undo button, select our image and move it to the center of the document.

Make Text Flow



Now we’ll change the way text flows around the image to give it a more magazine-like appearance. Note that below our selected image, there are three ways to get our image to interact with surrounding text: In line, Wrap text, and Break text. By default, Images are placed In line, meaning that they take up the entire vertical area “in line” with their height, preventing text from being placed next to them. To allow text to flow around our image, we’ll select our image, then select Wrap text. Note that text now flows to the right of our image in the empty space we created when we resized our image.

Because we have used the Wrap text function to force text to flow around our image, it is possible to experiment with different layouts by moving your image anywhere in the surrounding text. Try it. If you don’t like your layout choices, you can always undo them.

Save and Share Documents

Finally we get to one of the major differences between Docs and traditional word processors: the embedded ability to easily share your live document with others. Because Google docs reside in folders in Google’s cloud, you can provide people with access to those documents and even collaborate in real-time with others working on the same document. This is handy for group projects.

Constant Saving



Before we begin sharing a document, it’s important to understand that your document is constantly being saved. It is not necessary to use command + S or to select Save from the File menu to save a document, and in fact those options aren’t available in Docs. This may make you uncomfortable, but if you look at the top of the active document, every time you make any change, a message will appear: All changes saved in Drive.

Docs vs. Drive


What is Google Drive? It’s the online virtual hard drive that was created when you created your Google account. It’s the storage medium for the documents you create in Docs, and you can use it as a general-purpose repository the same way you might use a hard drive on your computer. The difference is that your Google Drive is available anywhere you have a computer or mobile device with an internet connection, rather than being located on a particular computer or server.

While you make changes to a document in Google Docs, you will see the All changes saved in Drive message disappear briefly. This indicates that your changes are being tracked and saved. When the message returns, the changes are permanently stored in Drive unless you explicitly undo them. This ongoing cyclic relationship between Docs and Drive is important to understand.


Prepare to Share


So far, we’ve covered how to get started with Google Docs, including getting a Google account, creating a document, importing and using fonts, and embedding and editing images. These are the foundations upon which you can build your own skill set with Docs.

The next step in using Google Docs is sharing your document with others. Giving others access to your live documents is one of the most powerful parts of Google Docs. Remember when you had to email versions of your documents as attachments to emails? Those days are over, thanks to cloud sharing.

By default, documents are not shared with anyone and are completely private. To share your document with others, click the Share button in the upper right corner.

People and Permissions



Clicking the Share button will bring up the sharing interface. To give one or more people access to your document, type in a recipient’s email address, then press the return button. To add additional recipients, repeat that process.


By default, people with whom you share your document will have Can edit access, meaning they will be able to make changes to your document. To restrict their access to your document, click on the Permissions menu in the upper right corner and select Can view, as we’ve done below:

When you have designated your recipients and adjusted their permissions levels, click the Send button. Note that this button is not sending your document, but is sending a link to your document residing on Google Drive.

Your recipients will receive an email that looks like this, allowing them to follow an embedded link to your document. Depending on their permission level, they can then view, edit, or comment on your document.

Take it to the Next Level

As you can see, Google Docs isn’t just a text editor, it’s a full-featured word processor, capable of layout and image editing functions. Its greatest advantage over traditional word processing applications may be its sharing function.
